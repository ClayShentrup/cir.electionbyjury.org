Against the Measure
Lane County Measure 20-373: Watersheds Bill of Rights
A 19-member citizen jury, demographically representative of Lane County voters, deliberated for three days on the proposed ordinance granting legal rights to all watersheds within Lane County. A majority found the measure's goals commendable but its legal framework constitutionally unworkable.

The Measure

Lane County Measure 20-373, the "Watersheds Bill of Rights," is a citizen-initiated ordinance placed on the May 19, 2026 ballot after petitioners collected more than 14,000 verified signatures. Chief petitioners are Michelle Holman and Kunu Bearchum of the group Protect Lane County Watersheds, with support from Community Rights Lane County.

What the Measure Would Do
The ordinance would grant legal rights to all watersheds, ecosystems, and natural communities within Lane County — including the McKenzie River, Willamette forks, Long Tom, and Siuslaw watersheds — to "exist, flourish, regenerate and naturally evolve, free from contamination and degradation." It would also establish residents' rights to clean and affordable water and to "sustainable recharge" of groundwater and flows protecting native fish habitat.

Any Lane County resident could file a lawsuit on behalf of a watershed against "corporate, government, or other business entities" found to impair these rights. Violators would be liable for full restoration costs plus a civil penalty of 1% per day of those costs from the time the impairment is reported until it is stopped. Lane County would be required to "enforce and defend this law to the fullest extent possible." The ordinance includes whistleblower protections and prevailing-plaintiff attorney fee recovery.
Key Watersheds Affected
Lane County contains portions of six major watersheds: the McKenzie River (sole source of Eugene's drinking water), Coast Fork Willamette, Middle Fork Willamette, Long Tom, Siuslaw, and numerous smaller tributaries draining to the Pacific Ocean. These watersheds encompass approximately 4,600 square miles across urban, agricultural, and timber lands. The ordinance would cover all of them.
Legal Context
This measure is part of a national "rights of nature" movement. The most prominent U.S. precedent is Toledo, Ohio's Lake Erie Bill of Rights (LEBOR), passed by voters with 61% approval in February 2019, then struck down by a federal judge in February 2020 as unconstitutionally vague and exceeding municipal authority. The court called it "not a close call." Ohio subsequently passed legislation prohibiting communities from enacting rights of nature laws. Similar measures have faced legal challenges in multiple U.S. jurisdictions, though the concept has been adopted in Ecuador, Bolivia, Colombia, and New Zealand at the national level.

The county is geographically diverse — about 55% of voters live in the Eugene-Springfield metro, with the remainder spread across rural timber, agricultural, and coastal communities.

The Deliberation

The panel deliberated over three days, hearing from witnesses representing petitioners, opposition groups, legal experts, scientists, farmers, and public officials. The deliberation followed standard CIR protocols: witness presentations, panel questioning, facilitated discussion, straw polls, and final vote.

Day 1 — Orientation, Testimony & Initial Positions

▸ expand
Round 1
Orientation & Measure Overview
Facilitators introduce the CIR process and read the full ballot title aloud: "Shall Lane County adopt the Watersheds Bill of Rights ordinance establishing rights for watersheds and allowing residents to sue for violations?" The panel receives the complete ordinance text (approximately 3,500 words). Several jurors note the document is dense and legalistic. Janet Fischer observes that the preamble reads more like a political manifesto than a legal instrument. Zach Moreno highlights the core innovation: granting "legal standing" to watersheds themselves.
Round 2
Petitioner Testimony — Michelle Holman
Michelle Holman, chief petitioner, describes why the measure is necessary: "When you go to your Congress people, when you go to your regulatory agencies, when you talk to corporate heads, they will tell you: this is all legal. The current system regulates the degree of allowable harm. We're saying harm should not be allowable." She describes invisible contaminants — herbicides and neurotoxins sprayed from helicopters over Lane County's timber lands that drift into drinking water. She references the 2011 EPA study near Triangle Lake that found atrazine and 2,4-D in every urine sample tested from area residents. Kevin O'Malley nods visibly — his family was among those tested. Holman explains the measure was drafted over several years with assistance from Community Rights Lane County and the Center for Democratic and Environmental Rights.

Diane Fossum asks whether the measure targets only corporate polluters or all activity. Holman responds that the enforcement mechanism targets "corporate, government, or other business entities," not individual homeowners. Fossum presses: "I'm a business entity. My farm is an LLC. Normal irrigation runoff could be called 'impairment'?"
Round 3
Petitioner Testimony — Kunu Bearchum
Kunu Bearchum, co-petitioner, focuses on invisible pollution: "When you think of polluted water, you think of discolored water, trash in the water. But there's a whole host of pollutants we can't see — chemicals and neurotoxins that the regulatory system permits and the Clean Water Act tolerates." Bearchum describes the measure as an exercise of self-governance when state and federal agencies have failed. She references the ordinance's preamble citing the Declaration of Independence and Oregon Constitution. Rachel Simmons asks how the measure squares with existing state and federal environmental law. Bearchum acknowledges the tension but argues that's precisely the point — current frameworks are insufficient.
Round 4
Opposition Testimony — Dave Addington, Oregon Farm Bureau
Dave Addington of the Oregon Farm Bureau presents the agricultural community's concerns: "It's going to be wide open. Anyone can litigate based on what they perceive as a harm to the watershed. Normal farming practices — scientifically sound, environmentally sound farming practices — if somebody disagrees with that. It's so broad that it's going to be whatever somebody's got a problem with." He walks through scenarios: a farmer spreads approved fertilizer and rainfall carries trace amounts to a stream. Under this ordinance, any resident could sue that farmer, claiming the watershed's right to be "free from contamination" was violated. The farmer bears restoration costs plus 1% per day penalties. The measure doesn't define what level of contamination triggers liability, what "naturally exist, flourish, and regenerate" means in practice, or what baseline the watershed should be restored to.

Mark Andersen asks about his cattle drinking from creeks on his property. Addington confirms this could theoretically be actionable. Sarah Nguyen pushes back: "Isn't the legal system already designed to sort out frivolous claims?" Addington responds that the cost of defending against even frivolous lawsuits can be ruinous for small farmers.
Round 5
Legal Expert — Professor Anne Rollins, UO School of Law
Professor Rollins provides a neutral analysis of the measure's legal standing. She explains the Lake Erie Bill of Rights precedent: passed by Toledo voters with 61% in 2019, struck down by a federal judge in 2020 on three grounds: (1) unconstitutional vagueness — the rights granted were too broad and undefined for anyone to know what conduct was prohibited; (2) exceeding municipal authority — a city can't override state and federal environmental permitting regimes; (3) due process violations — the lack of clear standards would invite arbitrary enforcement. "Lane County's Measure 20-373 would probably be challenged and invalidated on similar grounds. Our current legal system in the country really isn't set up to accommodate this legal theory. But it's also possible that somewhere, some judge could decide: 'Let's give it a try. Let's see what happens.'" She notes the measure uses the same framework language — "exist, flourish, regenerate and naturally evolve" — that the Toledo court found unconstitutionally vague. Amy Roth asks whether the measure could have value even if struck down. Rollins acknowledges the Toledo campaign did increase public awareness and may have influenced Ohio's subsequent $172 million H2Ohio water quality program, though causation is debatable.
Round 6
Panel Discussion & Initial Straw Poll
Heated initial discussion reveals deep divisions. Tom Whitebear speaks about the Kalapuya relationship with local waterways: "My ancestors didn't need a legal framework to tell them the river had rights. But I understand we live in a system that requires one." Jim Kessler responds: "I've worked timber for 35 years. We replant every acre we cut. We follow every regulation. This law would let any Eugene environmentalist drag me into court for doing my job." Theresa Kim says she's torn: "I moved here because of the beauty of this place. I want to protect it. But I also want a law that actually works."

Day 2 — Deep Dives: Science, Economics & Legal Viability

▸ expand
Round 7
Water Quality Expert — EWEB Source Protection Specialist
Dr. Marta Solano, source protection specialist with Eugene Water & Electric Board (EWEB), presents data on McKenzie River water quality. The McKenzie is one of the cleanest municipal water sources in the Pacific Northwest, partly because the upper watershed is largely publicly owned. She notes EWEB completed a comprehensive watershed study in 2012 concluding that forestry pesticide use is "not considered a likely threat to drinking water quality" in the McKenzie.

However, she acknowledges that other Lane County watersheds have different profiles. The Coast Fork Willamette, Long Tom, and Siuslaw watersheds contain significantly more private timberland with active aerial spray operations. Monitoring is sporadic because, unlike agricultural pesticides, forestry applications are infrequent and hard to predict. DEQ is "woefully underfunded" for comprehensive watershed monitoring.

Linda Clearwater asks whether current regulations are sufficient. Solano carefully states: "The regulatory framework provides meaningful protections, but there are gaps, particularly in monitoring frequency and enforcement resources." Kevin O'Malley counters with his experience: "In my neighborhood, we've seen drift events where people got nosebleeds, rashes, and their livestock had miscarriages. Tell me the regulations are working."
Round 8
Timber Industry Perspective — Oregon Forest & Industries Council
Sara Duncan from the Oregon Forest & Industries Council explains that aerial spraying accounts for approximately 4% of all pesticides applied in Oregon. Timber companies spray clear-cuts two to four times in the first few years after logging to protect newly planted seedlings from competing brush. She describes existing regulatory layers: Federal FIFRA requirements, Oregon Forest Practices Act buffers (300 feet from homes and water intakes for helicopter applications since the Private Forest Accord), and DEQ Clean Water Act permits.

She notes that statewide, pesticides were detected in only 8% of water samples from forested areas over the past decade. The 2020 Private Forest Accord significantly strengthened protections, increasing no-spray buffers and adding monitoring requirements. Duncan argues the measure would add litigation risk without improving actual water quality.

Patricia Hawkins challenges this: "But the Private Forest Accord was negotiated behind closed doors with the timber industry at the table. Of course it's a compromise they can live with." Duncan responds that environmental groups were also at the table and the agreement represented genuine concessions from industry.
Round 9
Constitutional Law Deep Dive — Oregon State Preemption
Professor Rollins returns for a deeper legal analysis. She walks through the specific constitutional vulnerabilities:

Vagueness: The measure grants watersheds the right to "exist, flourish, regenerate and naturally evolve" but never defines what conduct violates these rights. The Toledo court asked: would spreading a small amount of fertilizer violate it? Catching fish? Planting corn? Driving a gas-powered vehicle? The same questions apply here.

State Preemption: Oregon's Forest Practices Act, administered by the Oregon Department of Forestry, is a statewide regulatory framework. A county ordinance that effectively bans or penalizes activities specifically permitted under state law faces severe preemption challenges under Oregon's statutory framework, even though Oregon lacks Ohio's blanket preemption legislation.

Due Process: The 1% per day penalty structure — calculated on total restoration costs — could produce astronomical liability with no clear standard for what triggers it or how "restoration" is defined.

Rachel Simmons confirms: "In my professional experience, this is exactly the kind of law that gets enjoined before it ever takes effect. You'd see a temporary restraining order within days of passage."
Key shift: Megan Delaney, who entered the day undecided, writes in her notes: "I want this to work but the legal analysis is devastating. It's like writing a law that says 'be good' — everyone agrees with the sentiment but no one knows what it means."
Round 10
Rights of Nature Advocate — Center for Democratic and Environmental Rights
Mari Margil, representing the Center for Democratic and Environmental Rights (CDER), argues that legal evolution requires municipalities to push boundaries: "Every expansion of legal rights — from abolition to women's suffrage to civil rights — was once considered legally impossible. The existing system is designed to protect the status quo. It won't change unless communities insist on change." She points to international precedents: Ecuador constitutionally recognized rights of nature in 2008 and has enforced them in court. New Zealand's Te Awa Tupua Act gave the Whanganui River legal personhood in 2017 with a governance framework including appointed guardians. She argues the U.S. legal system can evolve to accommodate these concepts.

Janet Fischer, clearly engaged, asks: "But Ecuador and New Zealand did this at the national level with detailed implementation frameworks. This is a county-level ordinance that tries to override federal and state law. Isn't that fundamentally different?" Margil acknowledges the structural difference but argues that local action creates political pressure for state and national reform.
Round 11
Economic Impact — Lane County Budget Officer
Devon Ashbridge, Lane County Public Information Officer, relays the county's position: the county has not experienced a measure with similar language and has conducted no cost analysis, but implementing the measure — should it pass and survive legal challenge — would require research, new enforcement infrastructure, and potentially dedicated staff. The county's General Fund is already strained.

She notes the measure requires the county to "enforce and defend this law to the fullest extent possible," which could obligate the county to pursue litigation against violators at county taxpayer expense. If the measure is immediately challenged, the county would also bear the cost of defending it in court.

Steve Grabowski asks: "So we'd be paying to defend a law that experts say will get thrown out, and if by some miracle it stands, we'd be paying to enforce it?" Ashbridge declines to speculate on outcomes but confirms the cost implications in either scenario.
Round 12
Affected Resident Testimony — Triangle Lake Area
Jenn Ruppert, a resident of the Triangle Lake area in western Lane County, describes her family's experience with aerial herbicide drift: "September 2015. I heard a helicopter above a Weyerhaeuser ridge two miles from our house. I could see the drift coming down. It had a chemical smell, like ammonia. Within days, my children were coughing, nosebleeds, rashes, diarrhea. My rabbits started having miscarriages. Most of my beehives died." She explains that she filed complaints with the Oregon Department of Agriculture, which receives approximately three citizen complaints per year about all pesticide spraying statewide. Fines, when issued, range from $400 to $3,000. "The regulatory system is designed to let this happen," she says. "We have no real legal recourse."

This testimony visibly affects several jurors. Tom Whitebear puts his face in his hands. Theresa Kim writes quickly in her notebook. Even Jim Kessler says quietly: "That's not right. That shouldn't happen to anybody."
Key moment: The emotional power of Ruppert's testimony creates the most visceral pro-measure sentiment of the deliberation. Several undecided jurors visibly wrestle with the gap between "this law is probably unconstitutional" and "these people are being poisoned."
Round 13
Alternatives Discussion — What Else Could Be Done?
Facilitated discussion on alternative approaches. Rachel Simmons suggests that a narrowly targeted county ordinance — banning specific chemicals within a defined buffer zone around residential areas and water intakes — could potentially survive legal challenge, citing the Madison, Wisconsin fertilizer ordinance that the Toledo judge specifically praised as an example of "careful drafting." Paul Jimenez asks why the petitioners didn't pursue that approach.

Michelle Holman, re-called briefly, explains that specific chemical bans were considered but rejected because: (1) the timber industry would lobby to preempt any such ordinance at the state level; (2) new chemicals are constantly introduced that would circumvent narrow bans; and (3) the deeper problem is the legal framework that treats watersheds as property rather than as living systems with inherent value.

Janet Fischer sighs audibly: "So the answer is: we chose the approach that makes the biggest philosophical statement rather than the one that might actually protect people. I understand the impulse. But it breaks my heart."
Round 14
Mid-Deliberation Straw Poll
After extended discussion, the panel takes a second straw poll. The legal testimony has moved several undecided jurors toward No, while the Triangle Lake testimony has reinforced the Yes camp's resolve.

The Lake Erie campaign didn't win in court but it won $172 million for water quality programs." Round 15
Petitioner Rebuttal — Rob Dickinson
Rob Dickinson, spokesperson for the Yes on Measure 20-373 committee, responds to the opposition's legal arguments: "We fully expect to be dramatically outspent. But that has been true of many successful grassroots campaigns. Our strength is people, not big donors. As for the litigation concern — the legal system already allows people who believe they've been harmed to sue. We're not creating some new legal wild west." Round 16
Final Panel Discussion — The Core Tension
The facilitator frames the core question: Does the panel believe the potential benefits of this measure — signaling community values, creating pressure for policy change, and possibly establishing precedent — outweigh the risks of passing a law that legal experts agree is likely unconstitutional, would cost taxpayers money to defend, and could be used to target farmers and small businesses with vague claims?

Tom Whitebear: "I've heard all the legal arguments. I understand them. But the river doesn't care about constitutional law. The salmon don't care about precedent. Someone has to stand up and say: this is wrong. If not us, who?"

Janet Fischer: "Tom, I've been an environmentalist since before you were born. I chain-locked myself to a tree in the Opal Creek wilderness in 1989. But I've also learned that passing unenforceable laws doesn't protect trees or rivers — it just makes people feel good while the chainsaws keep running. I want to vote yes so badly. But I can't vote for a law that I know will accomplish nothing except spending taxpayer money on a losing court battle."

Kevin O'Malley, voice shaking: "My daughter had blood in her urine for three months after that spray event. Three months. And the state fined the company $1,200. Tell me the current system is adequate. Tell me to wait for the system to work."

Rachel Simmons, visibly moved: "Kevin, what happened to your family is unconscionable. I believe you completely. And that's exactly why I want a law that can actually help you — not one that gets thrown out in court while the companies pop champagne."

Key Findings

The panel agreed on the following findings after three days of testimony and deliberation:

Finding 1
Lane County watersheds face real contamination risks from aerial herbicide spraying on timber lands, agricultural runoff, and stormwater discharge. Current regulatory enforcement is limited: the Oregon Department of Agriculture receives approximately three citizen complaints per year about all pesticide applications statewide, and fines rarely exceed $3,000.
Agreed 19–0 (unanimous)
Finding 2
The measure uses language nearly identical to the Lake Erie Bill of Rights (LEBOR), which was struck down by a federal court in 2020 as unconstitutionally vague and exceeding municipal authority. The court called LEBOR's invalidation "not a close call" and specifically cited the phrases "exist, flourish, and naturally evolve" and "clean and healthy environment" as unconstitutionally vague. Measure 20-373 uses substantially the same language.
Agreed 18–1 (Whitebear dissenting on characterization)
Finding 3
The measure does not define what conduct violates watershed rights, what baseline a watershed should be "restored" to, or what level of contamination triggers liability. This creates legal uncertainty for farmers, ranchers, timber operators, construction companies, and Lane County government, all of whom could face citizen lawsuits under the ordinance.
Agreed 16–3 (Nguyen, Moreno, Roth dissenting)
Finding 4
If passed, the measure would likely face immediate legal challenge and would cost Lane County taxpayer money to defend in court. The county has conducted no cost analysis. If the measure survived challenge and required enforcement, it would necessitate new county infrastructure and staffing that the county has not budgeted for.
Agreed 17–2 (Roth and Blackwood dissenting)
Finding 5
Narrowly targeted local environmental ordinances can survive legal challenge when carefully drafted. The federal judge who struck down LEBOR specifically cited a Madison, Wisconsin ordinance restricting phosphorus-containing fertilizers as an example of valid local environmental legislation that "survived a similar lawsuit." More specific, carefully drafted ordinances remain an available tool for Lane County residents.
Agreed 19–0 (unanimous)
Finding 6
The panel found this to be a genuine conflict between deeply held values: the urgency of protecting Lane County's water from real contamination risks versus the legal reality that this particular ordinance is almost certainly unenforceable. Both positions reflect legitimate concerns. The majority concluded that passing an unenforceable law does not advance the goal of environmental protection; the minority concluded that the symbolic and political value of asserting watershed rights justifies passage even with legal uncertainty.
Agreed 19–0 (unanimous on characterization)

The minority's view:

Eight jurors voted YES, arguing that regulatory failure demands action even through imperfect tools, and that symbolic passage pressures state and federal reform.

Both sides agreed: Lane County's water deserves protection. This measure is not the right vehicle.

Methodology & Sources

Simulation Methodology

This simulation was generated by AI using the Citizens' Initiative Review methodology developed by Healthy Democracy, a nonpartisan Oregon nonprofit. Actual CIR panels are composed of randomly selected registered voters who are paid to participate, hear live witness testimony under oath, and are facilitated by trained moderators. This simulation applies the same structural methodology — random demographic sampling, balanced witness selection, iterative deliberation with straw polls — using publicly available information as source material.

This is not an actual CIR panel. No real jurors participated. All juror names, quotes, and deliberation content are simulated based on demographic data, published testimony, and reported arguments. The simulation is designed to illustrate what a CIR process might produce — not to substitute for one.

Jury Construction

19 jurors constructed to match Lane County registered voter demographics as of November 2024: 37% Democrat, 22% Republican, 34% Non-Affiliated, 7% minor party (281,184 total registered voters). Geographic distribution reflects the county's mix of urban Eugene-Springfield (~55%) and rural communities including Cottage Grove, Florence, Veneta, Oakridge, Junction City, Creswell, Lowell, and Dexter. Racial demographics matched to Census ACS 2023 estimates for Lane County (82% White, 9% Hispanic/Latino, 3% Asian, 2% multiracial, 1% Black, 1% Native American). Income distribution: $22K–$85K, median $48K (county median household income ~$55K). Ages: 28–72.

Design Rationale for 11-8 NO Vote

The 11-8 vote against the measure reflects several factors identified during research: (1) The Lake Erie Bill of Rights precedent is highly persuasive — a nearly identical legal framework was struck down as "not a close call," a fact that would be difficult for any jury to overlook after three days of testimony; (2) The measure's vagueness problems are objective, not partisan — even sympathetic legal experts acknowledge constitutional vulnerability; (3) Lane County is environmentally progressive but also contains substantial agricultural and timber communities directly affected by the litigation exposure; (4) The split reflects genuine value conflict — 5 of 7 Democrats voted YES despite legal concerns, showing the measure's emotional and moral pull, while 2 Democrats and 1 Green Party member voted NO specifically because they wanted a legally viable alternative, not because they oppose environmental protection.

A key design choice: the NO majority includes committed environmentalists (Janet Fischer, Pacific Green) and a public defender (Rachel Simmons) who explicitly stated they want stronger water protections but through a legally workable mechanism. This prevents the simulation from reading as a simple partisan split and reflects the genuine complexity of the question.
