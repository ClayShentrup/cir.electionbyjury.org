Proceedings — Five Days, Thirty Rounds

The simulated proceedings follow the structure of Oregon's Citizens' Initiative Review as adapted for a statewide Arizona measure: alternating presentations from advocates and opponents, neutral expert testimony, juror questions, and structured deliberation. Six rounds per day across five days. Advocates represent the strongest real-world arguments on each side, drawn from legislative testimony, municipal government statements, tax policy research, and public comment.

Day 1 Monday — Orientation and Opening Statements 6 rounds
Round 1 — Facilitator Orientation
Facilitator

Welcome. You have been selected as a virtual citizen panel to evaluate HCR 2021, a legislatively referred statute that will appear on Arizona's November 2026 ballot. Over five days, you will hear from advocates, opponents, fiscal analysts, municipal officials, tax policy experts, and affected residents. Your task is to produce key findings and an advisory vote to help Arizona voters make an informed decision.

Here is what the measure does. Currently, 70 of Arizona's 91 incorporated cities and towns impose a transaction privilege tax — what most people call a sales tax — on food purchased for home consumption. The rates range from about 1.5% to 4%. Arizona's three largest cities — Phoenix, Tucson, and Mesa — do not tax groceries. HCR 2021 would cap the grocery tax at 2% of the base price for any city or town statewide. Cities already above 2% would be frozen at their current rate but could never increase. Cities below 2% or without a grocery tax could only adopt or increase one with voter approval, up to the 2% maximum. The measure is retroactive to January 1, 2025, and includes a 24-month moratorium on new grocery taxes through June 30, 2027.

Joint Legislative Budget Committee — Fiscal Briefing

The JLBC estimates that HCR 2021 would reduce municipal transaction privilege tax collections by approximately $227 million annually by fiscal year 2028. That figure reflects the difference between current collections — about $196 million in FY 2024 across 70 cities — and what collections would be under a 2% cap, adjusted for projected growth in food prices. Forty of the 70 cities that currently tax groceries do not levy a primary property tax, meaning this revenue cannot be easily replaced through the state's other major local tax instrument. The state General Fund is not directly affected because Arizona eliminated its own grocery tax in 1980.

Round 2 — Pro Opening Statement
Rep. Leo Biasiucci (R-Lake Havasu City) — Sponsor, HCR 2021

Thank you. This is simple. Taxing the groceries that Arizona families need to survive is a regressive tax. It is wrong. When a single mother in Chandler buys a gallon of milk, a loaf of bread, and a dozen eggs for her kids, she should not be paying extra so the city can pad its budget. Everybody needs food. You can choose not to buy a television. You can choose not to eat at a restaurant. You cannot choose not to eat.

In 1980, Arizona did the right thing and eliminated the state sales tax on groceries. But we left a loophole that allowed cities and towns to keep taxing food. Forty-five years later, 70 cities still exploit that loophole. That means where you happen to live determines whether you pay an extra 2%, 3%, even 4% every time you go to the grocery store. A family in Phoenix pays zero. A family in Chandler — ten minutes away — pays 1.5% on top of already-rising food prices.

The original version of this bill would have eliminated the grocery tax entirely. We compromised. We capped it at 2%. We gave cities the ability to keep taxing groceries up to 2% with voter approval. We are not eliminating anything. We are putting a ceiling on it and letting the people — not city councils — decide whether to keep it. That is democracy. Since 2019, cities have seen a 60% increase in total sales tax revenue, largely because the Wayfair Supreme Court decision opened up online sales tax collections. They have more revenue than ever. They can absorb this.

Round 3 — Con Opening Statement
Tom Belshe — Executive Director, League of Arizona Cities and Towns

We share the concern about grocery prices. Nobody likes paying more at the checkout. But this measure is a permanent solution to a temporary problem, and the people who will pay the price are not who you think.

Let me give you the real numbers. Seventy cities and towns collect approximately $200 million per year from grocery taxes. For many of them, this is not a small line item — it is the backbone of their budget. The Town of Taylor gets 30% of its total revenue from the food tax. Bisbee gets 14%. Prescott Valley would lose $5.3 million — a 15% cut to its entire TPT revenue. These are not abstract numbers. They pay for fire trucks, police officers, road maintenance, parks, and libraries.

Here is what really concerns me. Forty of those 70 cities do not levy a property tax. In Arizona's system, municipalities have essentially two revenue tools: sales taxes and property taxes. If you eliminate or cap one, the only alternative is the other. Many of these communities — especially the rural ones — chose not to have property taxes precisely because the sales tax base, including food, was sufficient. Now the state legislature wants to take that away and leave them with nothing. What happens next? Either services get cut, or those towns adopt property taxes, which concentrates the burden on homeowners instead of spreading it across everyone who shops.

And consider this: 58% of Arizona voters live in cities that do not currently tax groceries. Phoenix, Tucson, Mesa — they opted out years ago. So this measure asks a majority of voters who are completely unaffected to restrict the revenue options of communities they don't live in. That's not democracy. That's the tyranny of the majority over local self-governance.

Round 4 — Pro Rebuttal
Rep. Neal Carter (R-San Tan Valley)

Mr. Belshe keeps saying "permanent solution to a temporary problem." But food prices are not going back to 2019 levels. The USDA reports that U.S. food prices rose 23.6% from 2020 to 2024. Families are not waiting for inflation to fix itself. They need relief now.

He talks about cities losing revenue. Let me talk about what cities have gained. Between 2019 and 2024, municipal sales tax collections in Arizona increased by more than 60% — not because of population growth alone, but because the Wayfair decision opened up billions in online sales that were previously untaxed. Every Amazon package, every online grocery delivery, every furniture order now generates TPT revenue. Cities have been swimming in new revenue. To say they "can't absorb" capping one component of their tax at 2% is not credible.

And the "local control" argument — let's be honest. If the City Council of Chandler raised grocery taxes from 1.5% to 4% tomorrow, would Mr. Belshe call that "local control"? Or would he call it gouging? The voter approval requirement doesn't take away local control. It gives it to the people instead of the council chambers.

Round 5 — Con Rebuttal
Ken Budge — Mayor, City of Bisbee

I'm the mayor of a town of 5,200 people in Cochise County. Let me tell you what $620,000 means to Bisbee. It means two police officers. It means keeping the road grader running. It means the community pool staying open in summer. We are not Phoenix. We do not have 1.6 million residents and a massive commercial tax base. We have retirees, artists, tourists, and a border economy.

The gentleman from San Tan Valley talks about the Wayfair windfall. Bisbee's online sales tax revenue from the Wayfair decision? Minimal. Our residents shop locally because we are hours from any major distribution center. The Wayfair bonanza went to Maricopa County, not to Cochise County.

I didn't come here to defend taxing food. I came here to ask you what replaces it. When Governor Hobbs vetoed the original version of this bill in 2023, she said, "This bill doesn't eliminate costs for our residents. It simply moves those costs around." She was right. If Bisbee loses $620,000, we will either cut services or we will have to pursue a property tax. And a property tax in Bisbee, where the median home value is about $170,000 and most residents are on fixed incomes, would hit harder than a 3% grocery tax on a $400 monthly food bill.

Jennifer Falk (J-08)
36 · ASU adjunct professor (public policy) · Tempe

What's the economic consensus on grocery taxes specifically? The CBPP is cited on both sides. Is there a clean answer on whether eliminating grocery taxes is good policy?

Facilitator (summarizing expert consensus)

The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, a left-leaning think tank, and the Tax Foundation, a right-leaning think tank, both recommend against taxing groceries. The CBPP calls it regressive because low-income households spend 11% or more of their income on groceries versus about 6% for high-income households. The Tax Foundation favors broad-based sales taxes on principle but acknowledges that grocery exemptions improve vertical equity. The key policy debate is not whether grocery taxes are regressive — almost everyone agrees they are — but whether the revenue loss to municipalities can be managed without creating worse problems. That is the empirical question this panel is evaluating.

Day 2 Tuesday — The Regressivity Question and Municipal Finance 6 rounds
Round 7 — Pro: The National Trend
Dr. Richard Auxier — Senior Policy Associate, Tax Policy Center (via video)

The national picture is clear. In 2020, thirteen states still taxed groceries. Since then, Oklahoma, Kansas, Virginia, Illinois, and Arkansas have all moved to eliminate their state-level grocery taxes. Alabama and Tennessee have reduced theirs. Utah voters approved a grocery tax repeal. This is a bipartisan consensus — these eliminations were signed by Republican and Democratic governors alike. Arizona is actually ahead of the curve because it eliminated the state grocery tax in 1980. What HCR 2021 does is extend that same logic to the municipal level.

The economic research is unambiguous on the distributional question. Sales taxes on food are among the most regressive taxes a government can impose. The bottom quintile of households by income spends roughly 33% of after-tax income on food. The top quintile spends about 8%. When you put a flat-rate tax on groceries, you are effectively imposing a higher tax rate on poor families than on wealthy families. This is textbook regressivity. The only debate is about how to replace the revenue — not whether the tax itself is unfair.

Round 8 — Con: Municipal Finance Deep Dive
Shawn Palmer — Mayor, Town of Taylor

I appreciate the professor's economics lesson. Now let me give you a civics lesson from a town of 4,500 people. Taylor's food tax is 3%. It generates roughly $620,000 a year. That is 30% of our general fund. We have no primary property tax. We chose not to have one because our sales tax base — including food — covered our needs. Our residents wanted it this way.

You know who pays our food tax? It's not just Taylor residents. It's the thousands of tourists and visitors who come through our town on the way to the White Mountains. They stop at our Safeway, our gas stations, our restaurants. They contribute to our roads, our water system, our public safety. That is how tourism towns work. A property tax captures none of that.

The sponsor of this bill is from Lake Havasu City, where the food tax is 2.35%. He's proposing to cap it at 2% — a modest reduction for his own town. But for Taylor, for Springerville, for Show Low, for Bisbee, for dozens of rural communities, this bill is an existential fiscal threat. We did not ask for this. The people of Taylor didn't petition the legislature to take away our revenue. The legislature decided to put it on a statewide ballot where 60% of the voters have no skin in the game.

Round 9 — Pro: The Equity Argument
Carmen Heredia — Director, Arizona PIRG (Public Interest Research Group)

I want to center the people this debate is actually about: low-income families. In Arizona, 14.1% of the population lives below the federal poverty line. That's over a million people. Forty percent of them are children. Many of these families live in exactly the cities that tax groceries — Chandler, Scottsdale, Tempe, Gilbert, Flagstaff, Yuma, Sierra Vista.

SNAP benefits — food stamps — are exempt from sales tax by federal law. But SNAP doesn't cover all food purchases for most families. The average SNAP benefit in Arizona is about $190 per person per month, which the USDA itself acknowledges is not sufficient to cover a healthy diet. Every dollar of groceries purchased above the SNAP benefit is taxed at the local rate. That's the cruelest irony: the poorest families, buying the cheapest food, at the most basic grocery stores, paying a tax that the wealthiest Arizonans in Phoenix and Tucson don't pay at all.

The geographic lottery of this tax is not defensible. Whether you pay tax on milk should not depend on which side of a city boundary line you live on.

Round 10 — Con: The Property Tax Trap
Nick Ponder — Lobbyist, League of Arizona Cities and Towns

Let me address the replacement revenue question directly, because it's the question that determines whether this measure helps or hurts low-income families. Forty of the 70 cities that tax groceries do not have a primary property tax. If those cities lose significant grocery tax revenue and cannot find alternatives, they face three options. One: cut services. Police response times increase. Road maintenance gets deferred. Parks close. Libraries lose hours. Two: adopt a property tax. This requires a vote, and it concentrates the burden on homeowners rather than distributing it across all shoppers. Three: raise the general sales tax rate on everything else. That is also regressive.

The proponents claim this is about helping low-income families. But if the result is that Bisbee, Prescott Valley, and Taylor adopt property taxes to replace lost revenue, then renters will face higher rents as landlords pass through the new property tax, and homeowners on fixed incomes — retirees, disabled residents — will face a new annual bill that they cannot avoid by shopping across town. At least with a grocery tax, you can shop at a store in an adjacent jurisdiction. You cannot move your house.

Round 12 — Expert Panel: National Evidence on Grocery Tax Elimination
Dr. Meg Wiehe — Deputy Director, Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (via video)

We model the distributional impact of tax changes for every state. In Arizona, because the grocery tax is municipal rather than statewide, the impact varies dramatically by location. In a city with a 3.5% grocery tax, a family in the bottom income quintile — earning under $25,000 — pays roughly 0.35% of their income in grocery taxes. A family in the top quintile pays about 0.06%. That's a 6-to-1 ratio of effective tax burden. By any measure, that is regressive.

However — and this is the critical nuance — the regressivity of the grocery tax must be weighed against the regressivity of whatever replaces it. If a city eliminates its grocery tax and replaces the revenue by raising the general sales tax rate by half a point, the net distributional effect is close to zero, because both taxes fall on consumption. If it replaces it with a progressive income tax — which Arizona municipalities cannot levy — the effect would be strongly progressive. If it replaces it with a property tax, the effect is mixed and depends on local housing markets. The worst outcome for low-income families is if the city simply cuts services without replacing revenue, because low-income households are the most dependent on public services.

Dr. Kevin McCarthy — President, Arizona Tax Research Association

Two factual points. First, state-shared revenue to cities is projected to grow from $1.05 billion in FY 2025 to $1.18 billion in FY 2028 — about a $130 million increase. That doesn't fully offset the $227 million grocery tax loss, but it's not nothing. Second, the 60% increase in municipal TPT revenue since 2019 that the proponents cite is real, but much of it reflects inflation — the same inflation that is making the grocery tax more burdensome. Cities' expenses have also risen 30-40% in the same period. The revenue growth does not translate to surplus in most cases.

Day 3 Wednesday — Local Control, Rural Impact, and the Voter Approval Mechanism 6 rounds
Round 13 — Pro: Voter Approval Is the Point
Scot Mussi — President, Arizona Free Enterprise Club

I want to reframe this debate. The opposition keeps saying this is the state overriding local control. But what is HCR 2021 actually doing? It's requiring that grocery taxes be approved by the voters of the city. That's not taking away local control — it's giving local control to the people who actually pay the tax, instead of leaving it in the hands of city councils who spend it.

Arizona has a long tradition of requiring voter approval for tax increases. Proposition 108 in 1992 required a two-thirds legislative vote for state tax increases. Cities routinely put bond measures and override elections before voters. The principle is simple: the people who pay should decide. If a grocery tax is truly popular and truly necessary, it will survive a vote. If the city council of Taylor can make its case to the residents of Taylor, the residents will approve the tax. That's how representative democracy is supposed to work.

The League's real objection is not about local control. It's that they don't trust their own residents to vote yes. And if that's the case — if the people who pay the tax wouldn't vote for it if given the choice — then perhaps it shouldn't exist.

Round 14 — Con: The Unfunded Mandate Problem
Mayor Shawn Palmer — Taylor (cont.)

Let me respond to Mr. Mussi directly. He says we should trust the voters. I do trust the voters — the voters of Taylor. But this isn't going on Taylor's ballot. This is going on Arizona's ballot, where voters in Phoenix and Tucson — who pay zero grocery tax — will decide whether my town can keep its revenue. 58% of the state's voters are unaffected. It's like asking people who don't own a car to vote on abolishing car insurance.

And the voter approval mechanism — let's think through the practical reality. Taylor has 4,500 people. Running an election costs money. Voter turnout in a special municipal election is typically 15-20%. So under HCR 2021, to maintain a tax that our residents have been paying for decades without complaint, we would need to spend tens of thousands of dollars we don't have to hold an election that 80% of our residents won't show up for. That's not "giving control to the people." That's creating an obstacle course.

Round 15 — Pro: The Human Cost of Doing Nothing
Maria Torres — Community organizer, Chicanos Por La Causa (via video from Yuma)

I work with families in Yuma County who are struggling. Yuma's food tax is 2.5%. That's on top of a general sales tax of over 10% when you add state, county, and city together. A family buying $800 in groceries a month — a family of five, that's realistic — is paying $20 a month just in city food tax. That's $240 a year. For a family earning $35,000, that's nearly a full week's take-home pay over the course of a year.

People ask me, "Is $20 a month really a big deal?" Come to Yuma and ask that question. Ask the mother deciding between fresh produce and canned goods because of the price difference. Ask the grandmother who clips every coupon in the paper and still comes up short at the register. The food tax doesn't just take money. It takes dignity. It says, "Even the food you need to live, you will be taxed for."

I've heard the arguments about city budgets. Those are real. But I need someone to explain to me why the poorest people in the poorest cities are the ones who should subsidize city services through the food they feed their families. Tax something else. Tax luxury goods. Tax commercial property. But stop taxing bread and milk.

Round 16 — Con: What Would Actually Help Low-Income Families More?
Dr. Dave Wells — Research Director, Grand Canyon Institute

I want to support Ms. Torres's concerns while challenging the assumption that HCR 2021 is the best way to address them. The measure caps the grocery tax at 2%. It does not eliminate it. A low-income family in a city that currently taxes groceries at 3.5% would save 1.5 percentage points — about $108 per year on $600 per month in purchases. That's meaningful, but it's modest.

Compare that to alternatives the legislature could have pursued. A refundable state grocery tax credit of $100 per person, targeted at households below 200% of the federal poverty level, would cost about $180 million annually and deliver roughly $400 to a family of four — concentrated entirely on the families who need it most. Higher-income families who don't need the help would get nothing. That would be progressive. HCR 2021, by contrast, gives the same percentage reduction to everyone, regardless of income. A family spending $2,000 a month at Whole Foods in Scottsdale saves more than a family spending $400 a month at Food City in Douglas.

Governor Hobbs made this point when she vetoed the 2023 version. There are better tools. The legislature chose the blunt one because it makes a good headline. "Arizona Caps Grocery Tax" polls well. "Arizona Enacts Refundable Grocery Tax Credit for Low-Income Families" doesn't fit on a bumper sticker.

Round 18 — Expert Panel: Comparative Evidence from Other States
Dr. Katherine Loughead — Senior Policy Analyst, Tax Foundation (via video)

I track state tax changes nationally. A few key comparisons are useful. Kansas fully phased out its grocery tax over three years, ending January 1, 2025. The state replaced the revenue by not reducing the state income tax as aggressively as originally planned. Oklahoma eliminated its 4.5% state grocery tax in August 2024 and absorbed the revenue loss into a strong state budget surplus. Illinois repealed its 1% grocery tax effective January 2026, funded by general revenue growth.

The Arizona case is different because the tax being restricted is municipal, not state. There is no direct parallel to a state restricting municipal grocery taxes by voter referendum. The closest analog might be California's Proposition 218 (1996), which required voter approval for most local taxes. Studies of Prop 218 found that it did constrain local revenue growth but did not cause the fiscal catastrophe opponents predicted, partly because cities adapted by diversifying revenue and partly because voters did approve many tax measures when given the chance.

The Tax Foundation's position is that groceries should not be taxed, but that municipalities should be given time and tools to adjust. HCR 2021's two-year moratorium on new grocery taxes through June 2027 provides some transition time, but the 2% cap takes effect retroactively to January 2025, which gives cities no transition period for existing rates above 2%.

Methodology and Transparency

This simulation was produced by an AI system (Claude, Anthropic) at the direction of the Election by Jury Project. It is not a poll, a prediction, or a substitute for human deliberation. It is a proof of concept demonstrating how AI-assisted citizen juries could inform democratic decision-making.

Jury construction: The 23 jurors were designed to approximate Arizona's demographic distribution by race/ethnicity (U.S. Census ACS 2023), party registration (Arizona Secretary of State, October 2025), religion (Pew Religious Landscape Study, 2023–24), age, income, housing tenure, and geography. Special attention was paid to including jurors from cities that tax groceries and cities that do not, as this distinction is central to the policy question. Jurors' occupations and personal circumstances were constructed to create realistic perspectives on both sides — including a town clerk from a grocery-tax-dependent community (Taylor), a cashier in a high-grocery-tax city (Douglas), and a parks coordinator in an affected municipality (Prescott Valley).

Witness testimony: All advocate and expert arguments are drawn from real-world sources: Arizona Capitol Times coverage, League of Arizona Cities and Towns testimony, JLBC fiscal notes, CBPP and Tax Foundation research, ITEP distributional analyses, Ballotpedia documentation, and public statements by legislators, mayors, and community organizations. Named real-world figures (Rep. Biasiucci, Tom Belshe, Mayor Budge, etc.) present arguments they have actually made in legislative hearings and public statements. Where testimony is synthesized or extended for the simulation, it is consistent with positions those individuals and organizations have publicly articulated.

Deliberation model: Jurors are modeled to update their positions in response to evidence and peer discussion, consistent with research on real deliberative panels (CIR Oregon, Citizens' Assemblies internationally). The rate of opinion change (4 jurors moving from undecided to YES, 1 from undecided to NO, 1 from leaning-YES to NO) is within the typical range observed in real CIR panels, where 20–35% of jurors change their initial position during deliberation. Juror reasoning reflects realistic cognitive patterns: analytical jurors (engineers, accountants) respond primarily to data; jurors with direct personal stakes (the town clerk, the cashier) weigh institutional and experiential knowledge heavily; jurors in scientific or policy fields show greater willingness to update on evidence.

Limitations: This is a simulation, not a substitute for real human deliberation. Real jurors would bring lived experiences, emotional responses, interpersonal dynamics, and serendipitous insights that no AI can fully model. The 14–9 verdict should be understood as one plausible outcome of an informed deliberative process, not as a prediction of how real jurors or real voters would decide. The simulation was designed to present the strongest possible arguments on both sides and to model jurors who are capable of genuine deliberation — meaning they can be persuaded by evidence but are not infinitely flexible.

No advocacy: The Election by Jury Project does not take positions on ballot measures. This simulation was designed to inform, not to persuade. The voter pamphlet argument above presents the jury's findings — including the concerns of jurors on both sides — as a factual summary for voters to evaluate alongside their own values and priorities.
